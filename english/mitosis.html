What is

MITOSIS?

Mitosis is divided into five main phases, which are only a didactic division, since mitosis is a continuous process.

Interphase

Interphase is the first phase of the cell cycle that comes before mitosis/meiosis. Its function is to prepare the cell to go through the process of division; interphase is divided into three stages, G1, S and G2.

G1

Transcription and translation of organelles, restarting RNA production and synthesis of proteins and cytoplasmic organelles.

S

Cell preparation/DNA duplication: DNA duplicates itself to form a new strand, thus generating sister chromatids.

G2

At the end, DNA condenses/spirals (coils), synthesizing proteins again.

Prophase

The first stage of mitosis. During this phase, some important changes are observed. Among the changes in prophase are the condensation of chromosomes, the disappearance of nucleoli and the beginning of the formation of the mitotic spindle. Mitotic spindles are formed by spindle fibers, which are bundles of microtubules. The microtubules that form the spindles are assembled in the centrosome, also called the microtubule organizing center. At this stage, we see that each duplicated chromosome is present as two sister chromatids, which are joined by their centromeres (a constriction located on the chromosome) and along their arms.

Prometaphase

In prometaphase, the nuclear envelope is fragmented and the chromosomes continue to condense. At the centromere of the chromosomes, there is a kinetochore (complexes formed by specialized proteins), which serves as a place for microtubules to bind.

Metaphase

In metaphase, the centrosomes are positioned at opposite poles of the cell and the chromosomes are gathered together in the so-called metaphase plate (equatorial plane). At this stage, neither the nuclear envelope nor the nucleolus can be seen.

Anaphase

Anaphase is the shortest phase of mitosis. It begins abruptly with the simultaneous separation of sister chromatids and is completed within a few minutes. At this stage, each chromatid begins to move towards the opposite side of the cell. This movement occurs as a result of the shortening of the kinetochore microtubules due to the loss of tubulin subunits. At this stage, we see the elongation of the cell and at the end of the anaphase stage we see that there is a complete collection of chromosomes at each end.

Telophase

In telophase, it is possible to notice the formation of nuclei because of the reappearance of nuclear envelopes around each batch of chromosomes. The envelopes arise from fragments of the mother cell's nucleus and other portions of the cell's endomembrane system. In addition to the nucleus, the nucleolus also reappears. At this stage, the chromosomes also decondense and the microtubules that are still present disappear. At this stage, mitosis is completed and the nuclei of the daughter cells enter interphase.

Cytokinesis

Cytokinesis is the division of the cytoplasm, which gives rise to two daughter cells. Cytokinesis usually takes place at the end of telophase. Cytokinesis is a process that occurs differently in plant and animal cells.

Somatic Cell

Somatic cells are any cells responsible for the formation of tissues and organs in multicellular organisms. This classification encompasses all diploid cells in the human body, which replicate only by the process of mitosis and are therefore not directly involved in reproduction.
